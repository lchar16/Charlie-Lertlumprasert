Lower Body Exoskeleton

Electronics and Controls Subteam Lead

Date: Aug 2019 - present

Location: Blacksburg, VA

Summary

This lower-body gravity compensating exoskeleton will support 50 pounds of body weight per leg while walking, climbing stairs, and performing basic, everyday activities. Our target customers are people with physical, lower-body impairments, as well as first responders and industrial workers. Current lower body exoskeletons are expensive, restrictive, stiff, and bulky. Our goals are to make the exoskeleton lightweight, low profile, and comfortable by matching a natural gait cycle.

The final design is a single-joint frame, single-motor actuation system, pictured below.

My Contributions

I worked on the electronics and controls side of the project, which is the actuator system – animation below!

The actuator system consists mainly of a motor and a spring. The gravity compensation comes from the motor compressing the spring, which provides an upward force on the body. So, selecting a motor that can provide enough torque and speed to mimic a natural gait cycle is very important and involves a lot of calculations. A big factor in how much torque and speed the motor needs to provide is the spring’s length and spring constant. Selecting the right combination of motor and spring can help minimize how much the motor needs to travel, and therefore power consumption.

Spring and Motor Selection

To start off, we gathered from published gait studies joint angle data as a function of the gait cycles for 4 movements: walking, ascending and descending stairs, and sitting to standing. These hip, knee, and ankle angles, combined with anthropometric data, allowed us to create animations on MATLAB of these movements. The walking animation of a person with a height in the 5th percentile is shown below.

From these stick figure calculations, a map of the body’s position at every point in the gait cycle can be obtained for all four modes of motion. Since the actuator will be attached on the upper thigh and the upper shin, the distance between the upper thigh and the upper shin as a function of the gait cycle can be calculated. With this information, along with ground reaction force (GRF) data, we can determine the motor’s required position throughout the gait cycle.

From the motor’s required position as a function of the gait cycle, we can also determine its required velocity and acceleration. Then, the moment of inertia of the motor and screw system was calculated to obtain the motor’s required torque. Finally, after plotting the speed and torque requirements on a graph, we can find out what the motor’s power, no-load speed, and stall torque should be! Here are the graphs I’m talking about.

That was a super simplified, so if you are interested in a little more detail on the calculations, here is a document that includes some derivations, as well as the MATLAB code. And here is a spring comparison document.



Electrical System

The electrical system consists of force sensors, distance sensors, spring potentiometer, battery, knee angle encoders, ankle angle encoders, motors, motor encoders, an Arduino, an IMU, a buck converter, and wires. The sensor selection process was mainly based on the information we knew about the frame, which kept evolving. Ultimately, we needed to select sensors that could be mounted safely, caused minimal protrusion, and that could be compatible with the microcontroller (5V or 3.3V input, serial or analog communication, etc.).

The most frustrating thing about sensors, is that they sometimes didn’t work the way we expected them to. For example, the force sensors we originally picked were force resistive sensors. After ordering them, waiting for them to ship, and testing, there just wasn’t a way to mount them on the footplate to get accurate readings. So, we had to find different ones.

"Foundational Experience" are just fancy words for everything that is on the interface of the Windows Operating System (OS): the Taskbar, the Start Menu, File Explorer, etc. So, everything you see on Windows is what my team is responsible for.

To clear up any potential confusion, our "product" is the Windows OS, and the "features" are things like the Taskbar, Start Menu, etc.

Project Overview

Again, because of NDA, I can't say much about what exactly I did here. Here's a brief overview:

My deliverable for the summer was a recommendation on whether or not we should add a new feature. At the start of my internship, the feature was not yet defined. So, I was also in charge of "spec-ing" it out: what it would look like, where it would be in the product, what kind of content should be on it, and what the user experience should look like. I worked with a team of two software developers, a data scientist, a designer, and a user researcher. To make this recommendation, there were two overarching questions I needed to answer:

  1. Will this new feature increase customer engagement and retention with the product?
  2. Will it increase subscriptions to the product?

To answer these questions, I needed to really understand our customers and how this new feature would influence the way they use and feel about our product. This involved conducting user research experiments and using the results from those experiments to inform the design of the feature. I also needed to make sure that each design modification was technically feasible.

The hardest part this process was orchestrating with the rest of the product team. I needed to make sure that every decision I made about this feature aligned with everyone else's feature goals and the product's goals as a whole. With such a large product team, I was constantly pulled in different directions and constantly overwhelmed with new information.

Once I spec'd out the feature to a certain level of detail, it was time to figure out its business impacts. This involved running Monte Carlo simulations using existing data to predict user engagement with the feature.

At the end of the summer, I had the chance to present my recommendation to Joe Belfiore, the Corporate Vice President of our division -- and my intern project was the only one out of 100+ that was presented to him!

Engineer Intern

Date: May 2017 - Aug 2017

Location: Sterling, VA

I worked under the Lean Leader, so all of my projects were process improvement efforts in order to drive up productivity. I was not allowed to take pictures, so all the text might be a little boring.

  1. Redesigned mobile units to enhance visual management and expedite assembly process for Dash 8 propeller parts.
    • The repair and overhaul process for propeller part involves disassembly of the part, putting all the pieces in a cart, and sending them through 4 different "gates" responsible for each step of the process. Because there are so many types of screws, bolts, and other small parts to keep track of, some inevitably would get mixed up or lost - decreasing productivity. In addition, at peak times, carts would overcrowd the working area, making it difficult to maneuver through the shop. I studied our shop workers at each gate to see how they interacted with the carts and used the information I gathered to design a new cart with shadow boards that mimicked the exploded view of a CAD model. This way, as the propeller part moved from tear-down, inspection, to assembly, there was a designated place for every piece. This standardization made the layout more intuitive, reduced the chances of lost/misplaced parts, cut down the square footage of the cart by 50%, and therefore increased productivity.
  2. Used Lean Principles and 5S to manage local storage to prevent material shortages.
    • During my first week of the internship, my mentor showed me the local storage in disarray, gave me a booklet on lean principles, and told me to implement a self sustaining system to organize it. This turned out to be the hardest project of the whole summer because it needed the cooperation of everyone on the shop. Because the LS wasn't organized well, shop workers spent a long time looking for some materials. Sometimes, we ran out of important materials, but no one knew to order more; sometimes, we had materials in stock, but people would order more because they couldn't find them, which resulted in old materials going to waste and shop workers waiting days for new materials to arrive. After reorganizing LS by applying all of the things I learned from that booklet, I implemented a new system that held everyone accountable for keeping LS neat and reporting any time we need to order new materials. To take it a step further, I made an interactive tutorial video with the help of shop workers. By doing this, I built relationships with people on the shop, which helped in implementing the new system. I showed the tutorial to everyone in the shop and checked LS every single morning. If I saw that something was done wrong, I tracked down the person to show them the right way. By the end of my 3 months, the shop workers rarely made any mistakes. This allowed everyone to be more productive because they no longer spent time looking for materials they needed and there were no more material shortages.
  3. Led process improvement efforts
    • Creative Wednesdays: every Wednesday, I led a 30-minute brainstorming session with workers in the Blade Room to come up with new ways to improve turnaround times. Many ideas were brought to life, such as creating a toolbox attached to the workers' chairs, changing types of sand paper, and using a heat gun to help remove blade de-icers.
    • Worked with quality engineer to streamline Dash 8 and C-130 blade repair process that saves company over 500 working hours and $65,000 per year.
  4. Created a method to track turnaround times at each stage of the repair process which saved 400 hours per year
    • As part of process improvement efforts, I was tracking how much time each worker spent at each step of the repair and overhaul process. Workers signed in to a job by scanning their badges at the start and finish of each job. Each day, the Operations Manager spent at least an hour to walk the shop and manually check where which blade and hub are in the repair or overhaul process in order to evaluate whether or not the products would be shipped back to the customer on time. I leveraged the information I was already tracking in order to create a digital chart, using Excel, of where every single blade in the shop was. Naval Research Laboratory

Chemistry Research Intern

Date: Jun 2015 - Aug 2015

Location: Washington DC

Project Overview

Again, because of NDA, I'll just give a brief overview of what I worked on.

My goal was to develop a new method to study platinum catalysts using the nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) technique. Nuclear magnetic resonance is an analytical tool that tells us about the structure of molecules. By performing experiments with the nuclei of atoms, the chemical environment of the nuclei can be determined from the data collected.

Since the NMR spectroscopy is used to determine the chemical environment of nuclei, it can be used to study how molecules interact with each other in a chemical reaction. Platinum catalysts are used for industrial purposes, such as autocatalysts and fuel cell technologies. However, platinum has a very broad lineshape, which means that there is a very wide range of frequencies that can excite platinum nuclei. A single experiment does not allow us to see platinum’s whole line. Consequently, we cannot determine the active structural features and optimize its use. To be able to see its whole line, we can vary the external magnetic field. Since we can only see a small section of its line, varying the external magnetic field will allow us to see different sections of the line, and we can then put together the pieces!

TJHSST Senior Research Project

Autonomous Underwater Vehicle

Date: Sept 2015 - May 2016

Location: Thomas Jefferson High School for Science and Technology

Project Overview

Autonomous Underwater Vehicles (AUVs) are self-powered robots which peruse waters independently and are capable of completing their surveyed missions without operator intervention. Underwater Gliders are a sub-class of AUVs, which operate by manipulating buoyancy, the tendency of objects to float in fluids, through varied models of internal actuators. The gliders in our experiment use internal tanks and pumps fashioned from syringes in conjunction with a set of external wings and a tail to dictate pitch (angle of ascent/descent) and direction.

Gliders move on a saw-tooth pattern, gliding downwards when their internal fluid volume is denser than surrounding waters, and upwards when their internal fluid volume is less dense than surrounding waters. Gliders’ ability to survey shallower waters than boats, and deeper waters than human divers or tethered vehicles can make AUVs are attractive options for oceanographic research. Underwater gliders are ideal due to their low cost, and low-power yet high endurance nature. Additionally, gliders have significantly greater range (hundreds or thousands of kilometers) and mission duration (weeks to months) than AUVs propelled by electric motors. Gliders can be outfitted with oceanographic sensors to measure various variables of the ocean. This data can provide scientists with information about ocean processes, like mixing; meteorologists with information about weather events, such as hurricanes; managers and business supervisors with information about water quality; and climate modelers with information regarding changes in the water over time.

My Contributions

I was in charge of the glider hull design and construction. Our glider is 30 inches long, 4 inches in diameter, and 22 inches in wingspan. The shape of our glider is inspired by a study by Parsons and Goodsons from Purdue University. The shape is called X-35 and is designed to be streamlined and low drag. Our glider is a somewhat elongated version of that shape. It has one-third of the drag of a regular torpedo shape, which is extremely efficient. In addition, it has adjustable wings to control the center of gravity, and consequently the angle of the dive. The farther the wing is from the center of gravity, the steeper the angle of descent and ascent. Although the wings were commercially bought from surfboard fins, it is extremely efficient. The winglets on the wings capture the lost vorticity and converts it to energy, which help reduce drag.

Our whole design was 3D printed. It took 100 hours to print the middle section and 40-50 hours to print the front and back section each. Our current design is printed with polylactic acid (PLA). However, PLA is not strong enough for our actual application. It is biodegradable, has a lower melting temperature, and is more rigid, making it harder for assembly. If PLA sits too long in the sun, it has a tendency to melt, which is not ideal for collecting data in coastal waters. Acrylonitrile butadiene styrene (ABS) would be more suitable for our goals. ABS is stronger, more flexible, and has a higher temperature resistance, and is not harmful to the environment.

An important part of building this glider was waterproofing it so that the insides do not flood with water and short the circuits. We waterproofed our glider based on a proven design by National Geographic using o-rings and screws. At the end of the nosecone and the backend of the glider, there are two grooves and series of screw holes. The o-rings are placed in the grooves and provide a tight fit when placed into the back half. The screws are used to add extra security to the whole design and hold everything in place. When the assembly experiences pressure, the outside pressure tends to crush the middle of the tube. When the center of the tube crushes, the screws outside the o-rings keep the tube from pulling away from the o-rings and breaking the seals, and consequently flooding the contents. Additionally, the middle section has stiffening rods and fin supports to further resist crushing by pressure. As the glider goes deeper underwater, the pressure increases one atmosphere for every 10 meters of depth. For our applications, the glider will go as deep as 200 feet, in which the pressure will be 20 times greater than the pressure at sea level.
