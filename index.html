Cloud and Artificial Intelligence Security Explorer Intern

Date: May 2018 - Aug 2018

I wrote service-layer threat detections for a new feature in Azure called Management Groups.

Management Group Overview

Microsoft's documentation

TLDR: Management Groups are containers for subscriptions. It allows you to organize your subscriptions in a hierachical tree structure in order to efficiently manage access, policies, and compliance.

Project Overview

Because of NDA, I can't say much about what exactly I did here. Here's a brief overview:

With the added convenience of Management Groups comes a much broader attack surface. Management Groups is still in preview right now, and there are a lot of things that will change before it rolls out publicly. I worked with many different service teams to protect Management Groups, both from the prevention and detection sides.

From the prevention side, I worked to integrate security features into the design of Management Groups. With the help of Azure Red Team engineers, I identified vulnerabilities in the current version of the feature and worked with the feature team to write threat protection plans and specifications.

On the detection side, I worked with the Identity Machine Learning team on writing cross-service detections for the feature. The main goal was to identify the hundreds, or even thousands, of attack scenarios in order to send security analysts alerts with a high signal-to-noise ratio.

General Electric Aviation

Engineer Intern

Date: May 2017 - Aug 2017

Location: Sterling, VA

I worked under the Lean Leader, so all of my projects were process improvement efforts in order to drive up productivity. I was not allowed to take pictures, so all the text might be a little boring.

  1. Redesigned mobile units to enhance visual management and expedite assembly process for Dash 8 propeller parts.
    • The repair and overhaul process for propeller part involves disassembly of the part, putting all the pieces in a cart, and sending them through 4 different "gates" responsible for each step of the process. Because there are so many types of screws, bolts, and other small parts to keep track of, some inevitably would get mixed up or lost - decreasing productivity. In addition, at peak times, carts would overcrowd the working area, making it difficult to maneuver through the shop. I studied our shop workers at each gate to see how they interacted with the carts and used the information I gathered to design a new cart with shadow boards that mimicked the exploded view of a CAD model. This way, as the propeller part moved from tear-down, inspection, to assembly, there was a designated place for every piece. This standardization made the layout more intuitive, reduced the chances of lost/misplaced parts, cut down the square footage of the cart by 50%, and therefore increased productivity.
  2. Used Lean Principles and 5S to manage local storage to prevent material shortages.
    • During my first week of the internship, my mentor showed me the local storage in disarray, gave me a booklet on lean principles, and told me to implement a self sustaining system to organize it. This turned out to be the hardest project of the whole summer because it needed the cooperation of everyone on the shop. Because the LS wasn't organized well, shop workers spent a long time looking for some materials. Sometimes, we ran out of important materials, but no one knew to order more; sometimes, we had materials in stock, but people would order more because they couldn't find them, which resulted in old materials going to waste and shop workers waiting days for new materials to arrive. After reorganizing LS by applying all of the things I learned from that booklet, I implemented a new system that held everyone accountable for keeping LS neat and reporting any time we need to order new materials. To take it a step further, I made an interactive tutorial video with the help of shop workers. By doing this, I built relationships with people on the shop, which helped in implementing the new system. I showed the tutorial to everyone in the shop and checked LS every single morning. If I saw that something was done wrong, I tracked down the person to show them the right way. By the end of my 3 months, the shop workers rarely made any mistakes. This allowed everyone to be more productive because they no longer spent time looking for materials they needed and there were no more material shortages.
  3. Led process improvement efforts
    • Creative Wednesdays: every Wednesday, I led a 30-minute brainstorming session with workers in the Blade Room to come up with new ways to improve turnaround times. Many ideas were brought to life, such as creating a toolbox attached to the workers' chairs, changing types of sand paper, and using a heat gun to help remove blade de-icers.
    • Worked with quality engineer to streamline Dash 8 and C-130 blade repair process that saves company over 500 working hours and $65,000 per year.
  4. Created a method to track turnaround times at each stage of the repair process which saved 400 hours per year
    • As part of process improvement efforts, I was tracking how much time each worker spent at each step of the repair and overhaul process. Workers signed in to a job by scanning their badges at the start and finish of each job. Each day, the Operations Manager spent at least an hour to walk the shop and manually check where which blade and hub are in the repair or overhaul process in order to evaluate whether or not the products would be shipped back to the customer on time. I leveraged the information I was already tracking in order to create a digital chart, using Excel, of where every single blade in the shop was. It featured conditional highlighting of WO numbers that were not on track to ship on time. In addition, it automatically updated at the end of each work day. The Operations Manager no longer had to spend more than an hour every morning and could get all the information she needed with a quick look at the chart I made.

Project Overview

Again, because of NDA, I'll just give a brief overview of what I worked on.

My goal was to develop a new method to study platinum catalysts using the nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) technique. Nuclear magnetic resonance is an analytical tool that tells us about the structure of molecules. By performing experiments with the nuclei of atoms, the chemical environment of the nuclei can be determined from the data collected.

Since the NMR spectroscopy is used to determine the chemical environment of nuclei, it can be used to study how molecules interact with each other in a chemical reaction. Platinum catalysts are used for industrial purposes, such as autocatalysts and fuel cell technologies. However, platinum has a very broad lineshape, which means that there is a very wide range of frequencies that can excite platinum nuclei. A single experiment does not allow us to see platinumâ€™s whole line. Consequently, we cannot determine the active structural features and optimize its use. To be able to see its whole line, we can vary the external magnetic field. Since we can only see a small section of its line, varying the external magnetic field will allow us to see different sections of the line, and we can then put together the pieces!
